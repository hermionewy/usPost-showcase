Gossamer Network

In 1848, the vast majority of what would become the western United States was controlled by native people. Less than half a century later, the region had been occupied by millions of American settlers. How did this happen so quickly, in the span of a single generation? The answer lies with an unlikely source: the U.S. Post. During the nineteenth century the federal government established more than 25,000 post offices in the western United States, building a vast communications infrastructure that helped knit together an otherwise remote region. This website maps the spread of that network between 1848 to 1895.

By Cameron Blevins, Steven Braun, Yan Wu

Despite what Hollywood might tell you, the history of the American West is not a story about rugged cowboys and brave pioneers. It is a story about big government. The most visible way in which the U.S. government (or, the American state) shaped the region was through coercive power: waging war against indigenous peoples, seizing their land, and forcing the survivors onto reservations. Coercive power was important, but it doesn’t tell the whole story. Settlers still had to occupy this plundered native land. To help them, the federal government turned to a much less visible kind of state power: the U.S. Post. As farmers, ranchers, and miners colonized remote corners of the West, they could depend on the world’s largest communications network to expand alongside them.
You can see the spread of this network using a dataset of post offices collected by the late postal historian and philatelist Richard Helbock. Each pink dot ⬤ represents one post office existing at that time.

The U.S. Post was expansive and fast-moving. Between 1848 and 1895 the federal government established roughly 24,000 western post offices. It was able to rapidly occupy some of the region’s least accessible areas, from the Chihuahuan Desert to the Central Basin to the Northern Rockies. Each blue dot ⬤ shows newly established post offices between 1848 and 1895.

The U.S. Post’s ability to rapidly expand into remote places helped settlers colonize the West while staying connected to the rest of the country. They used the mail to exchange letters with friends and family, subscribe to newspapers and magazines, or conduct long-distance financial transactions. The real power of the western postal network, however, came from its geography: the U.S. Post operated in more places - by far - than any other government institution.

Even more surprisingly, the western postal infrastructure was highly unstable. Between 1848 and 1895, thousands of post offices either shut down or changed names/locations. Some of these offices operated for just a few years or even months before closing down. These discontinued post offices are shown as empty red circles on the map: ◯.
The U.S. Post operated a gossamer network, rapidly spinning out new tendrils that were capable of melting away at a moment’s notice.

In most places, the U.S. Post did not own or operate its own infrastructure. Instead, it paid local merchants to distribute letters from their stores or contracted with stagecoach companies to carry bags of mail alongside their passengers. These temporary and part-time arrangements explain how the U.S. Post was able to expand so quickly, into so many different places - and why its network was so unstable. The federal government grafted its mail service onto an existing private infrastructure, creating a sprawling, decentralized system whose operations were tied to the fortunes of local businesses and communities.

Historical data comes with limitations, and this project is no different. Its first limitation is imprecise data. The Invasion of America project generously provided data about native land cessions mapped from federal treaties, executive orders, and government maps. These documents were intentionally vague, making it difficult to precisely delineate between unceded native land, government reservations, and colonized territory. To take one example: a treaty in 1855 established a common hunting ground for the Blackfoot, Salish and Kootenai (Flathead), and Nez Perce. Although classified as a “reservation” on this map, its borders didn’t mean much: by the 1870s thousands of settlers and goldseekers had occupied this area. The second limitation is missing data. Thousands of post offices from Richard Helbock’s dataset - roughly one out of every three - could not be located and therefore do not show up on the maps.

The U.S. Post and its gossamer network provided the underlying spatial circuitry of settler colonial occupation in the West. The following narrative maps where and how this network expanded. It begins with the United States laying claim to new western territory in 1848 and ends in 1895 with the region’s effective integration into the nation.
The narrative is broken into eight chapters, each of which covers a different six-year chunk and summarizes some of the major patterns from that period. You can read these sequentially or use the Table of Contents to jump to specific different chapters. Together, they reveal the hidden channel through which the American state consolidated its hold over the western United States.

1848-1853: FORAYS

Gold and conquest. This is the typical story of the year 1848. In January, James Marshall discovered gold outside of Sacramento, California. Nine days after Marshall’s discovery, the United States seized roughly half a million square miles of territory from Mexico in exchange for ending the American invasion and occupation of its southern neighbor. Parts of this land would later become the states of Arizona, Colorado, Nevada, New Mexico, Utah, Wyoming, and, finally, California and its newfound gold.

The discovery of gold and the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo touched off a global stampede to the American West, unleashing a wave of new settlement into freshly conquered territory. That, at least, is how the story goes. In reality, the overwhelming majority of the West was not yet controlled by the United States. It was native land. Thousands of different groups occupied the region, from Apache in the Southwest to Utes in the Colorado Plateau to Blackfoot on the northern plains. Native power effectively blocked Anglo-American expansion in the West. Between 1848-1853, the U.S. Post made only limited forays into the region.

The Gold Rush brought a boom of new postal infrastructure in Northern California between San Francisco and the gold fields of the Sierras. Much maligned at the time for its slowness, delays, and lost letters, the U.S. Post nevertheless managed to shuttle correspondence thousands of miles between remote mining camps and an American populace hungry for news and information about the gold rush. To the north, a second pocket of post offices appeared in Oregon, snaking south down the Willamette Valley alongside new farms and towns. Postal expansion along the Pacific Coast was made possible by a brutal campaign to exterminate and remove the area’s native inhabitants, paving the way for settlers to occupy this plundered land.

Two small clusters of post offices sprouted up in the western interior. The first centered on Salt Lake to serve new Mormon communities that laid down roots in newly created Utah Territory. The second came in New Mexico Territory, connecting the largely Hispanic settlements that clustered around Santa Fe.

The largest postal expansion came in eastern Texas, where thousands of planters had snatched up land in Texas following its secession from Mexico and annexation by the United States. Statehood in 1845 brought a flurry of new post offices that tied together the area’s cotton and slave economy.

Outside of these areas, the dominant pattern from 1848-1853 was the sheer quantity of western territory without any post offices whatsoever. The inability of the U.S. Post to expand across most of the West shows the difference between claiming territory and controlling it. The West remained the domain of native peoples.

1854-1859: EDGES

The United States had laid claim to the West - now what was to become of it? During the 1850s competing visions battled over the future of the region. No other issue proved more divisive than the question of slavery and its western expansion. Would this institution be legal in new western states and territories, or would this be a land of free labor?

Even as politicians debated the future of the West, the bulk of its land remained effectively off-limits to slaveholders and free laborers alike. Native peoples continued to block permanent white settlement in the western interior and confined growth to the edges of the region.

On the eastern edge of the West, the passage of the Kansas-Nebraska Act in 1854 unleashed both pro-slavery and anti-slavery settlers across the Missouri River eager to determine the future of these two new territories. As they clashed during so-called "Bleeding Kansas,” these new arrivals brought a wave of new post offices with them. Even after five years of expansion, however, postal infrastructure was still confined to the eastern prairies.

Postal growth in Texas was similarly constrained. Among other factors, the presence of the Comanche and Kiowa in western Texas limited the U.S. Post, as it largely filled in existing eastern areas of settlement rather than pushing farther westward across the state.

The presence of native peoples didn’t stop Americans from traveling across the West. During the 1850s tens of thousands of people traversed across several major trails, including the Oregon, California, and Mormon trails to the north and the Santa Fe and Butterfield trails to the south. Although a handful of post offices opened up at stage stops along these routes, most of the interior remained a place for Anglo-Americans to pass through rather than permanently settle. Outside of Mormon settlements in Utah Territory, a scattering of new offices in the Rio Grande Valley, and a few isolated military forts, native power largely stymied postal growth through the end of the decade.

Even in areas where the U.S. Government did manage to establish legal title over native land, its on-the-ground presence remained tenuous. In the Pacific Northwest, the government extracted territorial concessions from a range of different tribes during the 1850s. But its authority over this territory was unequal. It was only able to establish post offices near the coast, on land taken from the Umpqua and Kalapuya, the Chasta, Scoton, and Grave Creek, Kalapuya, the Nisqually, Puyallup, Steilacoom, Squaxin, S'Homamish, Stehchass, T'Peeksin, Squiaitl, and Sahehwamish, the Quinault and Quileute, the Klallam, and Duwamish and Suquamish. In the more rugged interior, Anglo-Americans failed to form permanent settlements and the U.S. Government failed to establish a civilian infrastructure, despite seizing land from the Yakama, the Confederated Tribes of Middle Oregon, the Walla Walla, Cayuse, and Umatilla, the Nez Perce, and the Flathood, Kootenai, Upper Pend d’Oreile.

Postal growth was strongest on the western edge of the West, up and down the Pacific Coast. Spurred by new gold discoveries in on both sides of the California/Oregon border, new post offices filled in the corridor between Oregon’s Willamette Valley and northern California. By the end of the 1850s, postal expansion in Washington Territory and southern California had extended a continuous, albeit uneven archipelago of post offices stretching from Puget Sound to San Diego.

Even as the federal government solidified its foothold on the Pacific Coast, it was approaching a crisis point in the eastern United States. By the end of the 1850s the slavery issue had fractured the nation along sectional lines, as Northerners spoke darkly of a “slave power” and Southerners began openly debating secession. When Americans filed to the polls in 1860 to cast their votes for President, the very future of the country - not just the West - was in question.

1860-1865: Artillery Shells

If American history is a drama, the Civil War is the narrative climax before intermission, the moment in which the United States teetered on the brink of dissolution. Typically this drama has been told from the perspective of the eastern United States, on battlefields like Antietam, Shiloh, or Gettysburg or in the halls of the nation’s capital. In this standard historical narrative, the western half of the country quietly waits off-stage, out of sight. But the American West didn’t stand still during the Civil War.

During the preceding dozen years, the U.S. Post had managed to carve out a Pacific foothold and a few scattered archipelagos of post offices, but was otherwise blocked from vast expanses of the western interior. In the early 1860s, that pattern changed. Even as the United States was putting down the Confederate rebellion in the South, it was taking aim at native sovereignty in the West. Between 1860-1865, the federal government seized millions of acres of native land.

Gold and silver drove much of the war-time settler expansion in the West. Historian Elliott West has described mining camps as “artillery shells” lobbed far ahead of more permanent kinds of settlement. It’s an apt image to describe the clusters of post offices that exploded across the western interior during this period.

The largest barrage rained down in Colorado Territory, where the discovery of gold in the Front Range in 1859 touched off a stampede of prospectors into the Rockies. Despite the fact that these mining camps were tucked away in mountainous terrain, the Post Office Department managed to open up dozens of new post offices up and down the Front Range. This burst of new post offices was driven by violent conflict. As prospectors clashed with the area’s native inhabitants, the U.S. Government to negotiated land cessions from Arapahoe and Cheyenne that paved the way for additional growth.

Colorado wasn’t alone. In Nevada, the discovery of silver in 1859 near the border of California led to a flurry of new post offices as prospectors staked out claims in what would become known as the Comstock Lode. Smaller silver discoveries in central Nevada produced two more concentrated bursts of post offices, while gold strikes to the north set off a scattershot of new settlements as prospectors flocked to the mountains. As in Colorado, the area’s mineral wealth was located on native land. Gold- and silver-hungry emigrants clashed with the area’s indigenous inhabitants and pressured the federal government to extract new land cessions from the Shoshone, Nez Perce, and Washoe.

Older areas of Anglo-American settlement continued to expand. Dozens of new post offices sprouted up in Northern California, Willamette Valley, and Puget Sound during the Civil War. In the interior, Mormon farming and ranching communities continued to fill in a sweeping arc down the heart of Utah Territory, many of them located on recently seized Uintah Ute land.

Finally, on the eastern edge of the western United States settlers inched westward across the eastern prairies of Kansas and Nebraska, establishing dozens of new post offices in their wake. Despite the artillery shells post offices that had peppered the West over the preceding years, most of the region still remained under native control.

The Civil War years laid the groundwork for future growth in the West. Republicans used a commanding majority in Congress to pass a series of new laws targeted at promoting settlement and investment in the western United States. Much of this legislation centered on the distribution of public land: to individual settlers (the Homestead Act), state colleges (the Morrill Act), and railroad companies (the Pacific Railway Acts). The effects of this war-time legislation would reverberate across the West for years to come

1866-1871: CORRIDORS

In the aftermath of the Civil War, a bloodied nation turned westward. During these years the Republican Party’s seeds for a government-backed expansion project began to bloom, most famously with the completion of the transcontinental railroad in 1869. Made possible by vast public land grants to the Union Pacific and Central Pacific Railroads, the transcontinental embodied a major pattern of expansion during this period: corridors.

In the wake of the Civil War, the U.S. Army mobilized troops westward to fight native peoples. Through warfare and treaties, the United States seized tens of millions of acres of native land during these years. The U.S. Post then helped the federal government consolidate its hold on conquered western territory, opening some 2,000 new post offices in the West between 1866 and 1871.

Recently seized native land paved the way for the construction of the transcontinental corridor, including the Arapahoe and Cheyenne in 1861, the Shoshone in 1863, the Washoe in 1865, and the Shoshone and Bannock in 1868. As workers laid down nearly 1,700 miles of railroad tracks through this corridor, the U.S. Post extended a string of post offices alongside them.

The impact of the transcontinental railroad on Anglo-American settlement rippled out from either end of the route. In the west, California’s postal system surged in the immediate aftermath of the transcontinental railroad, much of it along a web of new railroad spokes that radiated out from Sacramento and the Bay Area.

In the east, a wave of settlers flooded the prairies of southeastern Nebraska and eastern Kansas, many of them forming communities alongside existing and planned railroad lines. They brought the nation’s postal system with them: by 1870-1871, Kansas was adding more new post offices than any other state in the country.

Other sorts of corridors crystalized during the late 1860s. A number of transportation routes began to radiate out from Denver, Colorado, establishing the young city as the central hub of the Mountain West. New mail routes and post offices unfurled along railroad branch lines and the newly completed Kansas Pacific Railroad.
Farther south, a band of new post offices swept down the eastern face of the Rocky Mountains and the Sangre de Cristo Mountains to Santa Fe, New Mexico before narrowing down to the Rio Grande River artery that ran south to El Paso, Texas, and the Mexican border.

Elsewhere, Americans hungry for gold and silver ventured into remote areas of the western interior. These mining ventures produced a now-familiar artillery-shell pattern of postal infrastructure. The largest barrage came in western Montana, where the discovery of gold led to the establishment of some eighty new post offices between 1866-1871. Compared to the earlier mineral rushes in California and Colorado, this mining boom has received comparatively little attention. But postal growth shows just how quickly these gold strikes transformed the southwestern corner of Montana. In Nevada, silver prospectors and mining companies fanned out across the state and dragged the nation’s postal network with them, establishing ninety new offices in the span of six years.

Despite the best efforts of the U.S. Government, large pockets of the West continued to remain off-limits to Anglo-American colonization through the end of the 1860s. The most extreme example came in the Powder River Country of northern Wyoming, southern Montana, and western Dakota Territory. There, an alliance led by seven Lakota tribes had effectively repelled the U.S. Army and extracted sweeping concessions from the federal government, including the expulsion of troops and settlers from the area. The lack of postal infrastructure across this huge tract of land tells a story about the persistence of native power and the geographical limits of settler expansion. That story was about to change.

1872-1877: COLONIZATION

During the 1870s the U.S. Government broke down the walls of native sovereignty across the western interior. It did so through violence, as the U.S. Army waged wars of conquest against the region’s native peoples and forced the survivors to relocate onto government reservations. But dispossession took place through quieter channels as well. The U.S. Post’s ability to rapidly expand into new places greased the wheels for settlers to colonize conquered native land.

The most notorious episode of dispossession unfolded in the Black Hills, a sacred area of land that was part of the Great Sioux Reservation. During the mid-1870s, prospectors illegally encroached into the Black Hills in search of recently discovered gold. When a group of Lakota and their allies moved to protect their land, the U.S. Army used it as a pretext for war in 1876.

Congress officially annexed territory that contained the Black Hills in February, 1877, and within a matter of months a dozen new post offices were up and running. This rapid expansion transformed the space of the area from a sacred site - He Sapa or Paha Sapa in Lakota - into a colonized landscape of settlements, mining claims, and post offices.

Again and again, American settlers were able to mobilize the U.S. Post to help them colonize native land. In the southwestern corner of Colorado, white settlers had been banned from the San Juan Mountains as part of an 1868 agreement with Ute leaders. Almost immediately, prospectors started making illegal forays in search of gold and silver. In the spring of 1874 government officials negotiated the cession of 3.7 million acres of Ute land. Miners and settlers immediately swarmed into this otherwise remote area, confident that the U.S. Post’s connective infrastructure would expand alongside them.

In the Pacific Northwest, a cluster of new post offices sprouted up in eastern Washington and northern Idaho, extending across territory that the U.S. Government had seized from the Methow and Okanagan and Cour d’Alene groups. A similar pattern unfolded in northern California and southern Oregon, where the government had seized land from the Klamath, Modoc, and Yahooskin band of Snake Indians in the 1860s. In 1872-1873, the U.S. Army put down a small-scale insurgency of Modocs, paving the way for accelerated postal expansion in the area.

The U.S. Government forced surviving Modocs from the 1872-1873 war to relocate to Indian Territory, part of a wider campaign that began in the 1830s to violently remove native people from their land. By the late 1870s, the American state had confined some two-dozen groups within a patchwork of government-run reservations in Indian Territory. Many suffered from a chronic lack of food, supplies, and other government-provided resources. This included postal infrastructure, as the otherwise dense postal coverage in surrounding states all but ground to a halt at the borders of Indian Territory. In a pattern that continues to this day, white settlers could rely on an expansive government service while native people could not.

Despite a national depression that began in 1873, colonizers and post offices continued to expand in familiar patterns. Mining ventures set off bursts of postal artillery shells in Idaho and Montana, Nevada, and Arizona. Postal corridors sprouted up alongside railroad lines like the Southern Pacific in California or the Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway in Kansas and Colorado. And on the eastern edge of the region, thousands of farmers and ranchers established new towns and post offices as they pushed westward across the Great Plains into drier and drier climates. By 1877, the region stood poised to turn the page on a new era of development.

1878-1883: Churn

The period 1878-1883 was a hinge moment for the western United States. The prior three decades had revolved around the conquest and occupation of territory, as the U.S. government seized native land for American settlers to colonize. Violence and dispossession would continue, but by the late 1870s the U.S. Government had effectively conquered the region. Increasingly, it turned towards integrating this plundered territory into the nation. The U.S. Post was the quiet government channel that facilitated this process.

Western land, minerals, crops, and livestock fed the engines of a rapidly industrializing nation. But the region’s dependence on extractive industry and eastern capital made for a volatile economy. In fact, no other region has become so synonymous with a “boom-and-bust” economy, as periods of spectacular growth - mining strikes, cattle rushes, real-estate bubbles - were followed by ecological and financial collapse. The late 1870s witnessed one of these classic western booms. As the nation recovered from an economic depression, people and capital surged westward between 1878-1883. So, too, did the U.S. Post, establishing more than 5,000 new post offices to serve the region’s new farms, mines, ranches, and railroads.

"Boom-and-bust" is a sequential model: first the bender, then the hangover. But a closer look at the western postal network during 1878-1883 shows a different sort of pattern. Postal growth and postal failure were simultaneous, not sequential. Instead of a distinctive “boom” phase followed by a “bust” phase, both post office openings and post office closings rocketed upwards during 1878-1883. It was not boom then bust; it was boom with bust. In a word: churn.

On the central and southern plains, aspiring homesteaders and a “ranch rush” of cattle and sheep moved into drier and drier environments. The U.S. Post moved with them, continuing to thicken its network in central Texas and western Kansas. As aridity increased, so did instability. Much of this expansion followed a “churn” model of growth, as more than one thousand post offices in these two states either shut down or changed names or locations during this six-year period. Unstable as it may have been, this expansionary infrastructure provided settlers with a vital public service - one that continued to wither away at the borders of Indian Territory.

To the north, the late 1870s kicked off a frenzied rush of settlers across the northern plains known as the “Great Dakota Boom.” The territory’s postal network underwent its own boom during these years, as the number of post offices in Dakota more than tripled between 1878-1883. Railroads like the Northern Pacific fueled some of this growth. So, too, did native dispossession. In 1879, President Rutherford Hayes issued an executive order opening up land that had been reserved for the Lakota Sioux. The following year, officials forced additional cessions from the Arikara, Gros Ventre, and Mandan. In both cases, the U.S. Post's expansionary gossamer network rapidly occupied this plundered territory.

Conquest and colonization continued in western Colorado, where a splinter group of White River Utes launched an insurgency against local government officials in 1879. The federal government used this as a pretext to expel not only the White River Utes, but also the Uncompahgre and Southern Utes, all of whom ceded some six million combined acres of territory. Congress opened up this area for white settlement in 1882, and over the next eighteen months dozens of new post offices swept across plundered land. The U.S. Post once again helped the American state transform indigenous territory into a colonized landscape.

The U.S. Post facilitated a churning brand of colonization that reached its peak in the Mountain West. From 1878-1883, mines and railroads spurred much of the area’s postal growth. Silver mining proliferated in Colorado and Arizona, spurred in part by the Bland-Allison Act of 1878 that required the U.S. Treasury to buy, mint, and circulate silver currency. Other post offices opened alongside new railroad lines and branches in Arizona, New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado. Much of this new postal infrastructure proved remarkably ephemeral. Nearly four hundred post offices either closed or changed names/locations in Arizona, Colorado, New Mexico, and Utah between 1878-1883; of these, half had been in operation for less than two years.

Industrial expansion similarly shaped postal growth in the northern Mountain West. By 1883, new railroads were carrying gold, silver, lead, timber, and livestock. A burst of new postal infrastructure helped connect otherwise remote mining camps, sawmills, and ranching communities. Once again, growth went hand-in-hand with failure: more than two hundred post offices shut down in Idaho, Montana, Oregon, Washington, and Wyoming during this six-year-period.

1884-1889: Divergence

The churning growth of the 1870s and 1880s led to enormous disparities, bringing fantastic wealth to particular financiers and industrialists while the majority of westerners struggled to cope with the region’s volatile economy. This gap would become even more accentuated in the coming years. Between 1884-1889, industrial development and settler colonization continued to extend the western postal network across the region. However, this period was marked by growing divergence: some parts of the West accelerated while others slowed to a crawl.

During the late 1880s the engine of postal growth migrated outwards from interior to the edges of the region: along the Pacific coast, across a northern band, and in the plains and prairies.

The same mountain states and territories that had driven much of the postal expansion of 1878-1883 experienced a major slowdown between 1884-1889. Industrial expansion - especially in mining and railroads - decelerated in the area during second half of the 1880s, triggered in part by a broader dip in the national economy. In the north, the ongoing construction of the Greater Northern Railroad and branch lines of the Northern Pacific Railroad connected new lumber mills and mines - all of which fueled the growth of a larger postal corridor across northern Dakota, Montana, Idaho, and Washington.

To the west, postal growth along the Pacific accelerated even more dramatically. California, in particular, experienced a surge of new post offices during this period. Southern California was at the epicenter of this growth, driven by a wave of real estate speculation that enveloped Los Angeles and San Diego and peaked in 1887-1888. Despite its speculative nature, this growth proved surprisingly stable: 70% of new California post offices would survive for at least ten years.

Stability was in much shorter supply on the Central Plains, where the churning pattern of postal growth reached new heights during the late 1880s. Thousands of settlers filed for land patents along new railroad lines that brought them steadily into drier and drier climates in western Kansas and Nebraska. But twin disasters struck in 1886-1887: a crippling drought followed by one of the worst winters in living memory. Farming and ranching ventures cratered and over this period hundreds of communities and post offices dissolved across the Central Plains.

The pattern of divergence crystallized at the borders of Indian Territory. As unstable as Kansas’s postal infrastructure may have been, its residents could still rely on an expansive communications infrastructure. Native residents in Indian Territory could not. This disparity came into even starker relief in early 1889, when the federal government negotiated a large land cession from the Muscogee that opened up so-called “Unassigned Lands” to settlers. When the dust cleared, thousands of settlers had claimed nearly two million acres of land in one of the most famous land rushes in American history. Within a matter of months, the Post Office Department established a dozen new post offices in an area that it had previously ignored.

1890-1895: Integration

Adding some By the 1890s, the western United States was effectively integrated into the nation both politically, economically, and culturally. A region that had once been remote and isolated was now increasingly connected to the wider world.

In just eight months spanning November 1889 to July 1890, Congress created six new western states: North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, Washington, Idaho, and Wyoming. Political integration went hand-in-hand with economic integration, as western ore, timber, crops, and livestock traveled across a growing regional railway network to eastern cities and factories. And the U.S. Post continued to knit together a national system of information, establishing some five thousand new western post offices between 1890-1895.

In the Northwest, the admission of Washington, Idaho, Montana, and Wyoming as new states and the completion of the Great Northern Railway in 1893 - the nineteenth century’s last transcontinental railroad - led to a surge of new post offices. To the north, political integration brought about a similar loss of land for native peoples living in the Great Sioux Reservation. On February 22, 1889, Congress voted to divide Dakota Territory in half and admit North and South Dakota as two new states. Eight days later, those same Congressmen voted to split apart the Great Sioux Reservation within Dakota into six smaller agencies and open up nine million acres of land to white settlers. As soon as this legislation went into effect in early 1890, a string of post offices began to carve a corridor across this territory.

Western integration galloped onwards in the Central Plains with the incorporation of Oklahoma Territory in 1890. But Oklahoma wasn’t conjured out of thin air; it was cleaved off from Indian Territory. Oklahoma Territory grew even larger in 1893 when the U.S. Government forced the Cherokee to sell their rights to some eight million additional acres known as the “Cherokee Outlet.” At noon on September 16, 1893, government officials opened up this area to non-native settlers. Within a matter of hours, thousands of people had laid claim to plots of land. And within a matter of months, the U.S. Post was bringing them their mail across a dense network of new post offices. As it had for decades, native dispossession and postal expansion went hand-in-hand.

Immediately to the north, Kansas and Nebraska experienced the downsides of economic integration. Farmers faced a deflationary economy, crippling drought, and, beginning in 1893, one of the worst economic depressions in American history. Postal growth nosedived and Kansas and Nebraska’s post office coverage began to steadily shrink. What had once been an engine for the west’s roiling growth had turned into an area littered with failed communities. This landscape of postal collapse was the backdrop for the concurrent rise of the People’s Party, an agrarian political insurgency that originated on the central plains and swept across the western United States during the early 1890s.
